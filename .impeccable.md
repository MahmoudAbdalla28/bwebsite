## Design Context

### Users
Enterprise security buyers (CTOs, CROs, Managing Directors) referred through AON insurance broker. They arrive warm — already briefed on the need for AI agent risk telemetry. Their context is evaluating whether Bastion is credible enough to deploy on their infrastructure and generate data their carrier will accept. They are comparing against AIUC and Acuvity.

### Brand Personality
**Quiet, precise, foundational.**

Bastion doesn't announce itself loudly. It presents itself the way infrastructure should — as something you trust because of how it's built, not how it's marketed. The site should feel like opening a well-made technical document from a company that's been doing this for decades, even though it hasn't.

### Aesthetic Direction

**References:**
- **AIUC** — Confident restraint. Large serif/sans headings, generous whitespace, interactive diagrams that feel architectural. Nothing shouts.
- **Cisco** — Editorial illustrated feel. Flowing dashed lines, narrative visual storytelling, not wireframe energy.
- **Qover** — Soft gradients, layered depth, orbital/radial compositions for security visuals. Feels like a product, not a diagram.

**Anti-patterns (what Bastion should NOT look like):**
- Technical documentation dressed as a website (boxy SVG diagrams with monospace labels crammed into gray rectangles)
- Startup landing pages with gradient CTAs and "not X, not Y, not Z" defensive copy
- Anything that feels like it's trying to convince — the site should present, not persuade

**Theme:** Light mode. Clean white backgrounds, blue accent palette, soft depth through subtle gradients and layered elements.

**Font:** Inter (system-ui fallback). Used for both headings and body.

### Design Principles

1. **Let the architecture speak.** Diagrams and visuals should feel like considered architectural drawings — not wireframes. Use whitespace, proportion, and subtlety to communicate sophistication. If a diagram looks like it could be in a README, it's wrong.

2. **Confidence through restraint.** The less you say, the more authority you project. Short copy, large type, generous spacing. Every word earns its place. Defensive language ("no X, no Y") signals insecurity — state what it does, not what it doesn't.

3. **Consistency is credibility.** Stay within the blue palette. No surprise colors. No jarring visual shifts between sections. The site should feel like one continuous experience, not a collection of components.

4. **Visualize, don't explain.** Data flow, security layers, deployment architecture — show these through visual narrative (flowing lines, layered compositions, interactive elements), not through labeled boxes and bullet lists.

5. **Enterprise-grade means editorial-grade.** The quality bar is set by the companies Bastion competes with. Every section should look like it was designed by a team, not generated by a tool. Typography, spacing, and proportion matter at the pixel level.
